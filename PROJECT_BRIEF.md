# nem-demand-forecast: Project Brief

## 1. Motivation and audience

This is a public portfolio project. Its purpose is to demonstrate mathematically rigorous, probabilistic time-series modelling to a technical audience in the Australian energy sector, in particular hiring teams for data-science and statistical-modelling roles in the National Electricity Market (NEM). The project should read as the work of a practitioner who is fluent in both classical and Bayesian methods and who treats forecasting as a problem of calibrated uncertainty rather than point prediction.

The headline narrative is a like-for-like comparison of inference strategies on a single, well-specified generative model. A Bayesian structural time-series (BSTS) model is fitted two ways, by automatic-differentiation variational inference (ADVI) and by Hamiltonian Monte Carlo with the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS), and both are benchmarked against a strong classical baseline. The comparison isolates the inference algorithm rather than confounding it with model differences, and it is judged on probabilistic accuracy, calibration and computational cost.

## 2. Objectives

Forecast NSW1 electrical demand, day-ahead, at half-hourly resolution, and compare:

- a classical baseline: dynamic harmonic regression with ARIMA errors;
- the BSTS fitted by ADVI;
- the same BSTS fitted by NUTS.

Demonstrate, in notebooks:

- exploratory analysis and principled data cleansing, producing the train, validation and test sets;
- ELBO convergence for ADVI, decomposed into its energy and entropy terms;
- probabilistic accuracy via the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS), computed from the Bayesian posterior predictive and from the classical predictive;
- the full model comparison, including a posterior comparison of ADVI against NUTS;
- fit time and forecast time for every model;
- an additional comparison of NUTS fit time with and without an ADVI warm start.

## 3. Conventions and constraints (apply everywhere)

- **Language:** British English in all prose, comments, docstrings, commit messages and documentation.
- **Punctuation:** no em-dashes anywhere. No Oxford comma (write "a, b and c").
- **Primary language:** Python. **Probabilistic framework:** NumPyro on the JAX backend.
- **Human-authored:** code must read as written by a person. No references to AI assistants anywhere, in code, comments, docstrings, notebooks, commit messages, READMEs or elsewhere. No "generated by" footers, no AI-style boilerplate, no over-explanatory narration. Comments explain non-obvious decisions and intent, not the obvious mechanics of each line.
- **Memory discipline is first-class.** Manage RAM and device memory deliberately: explicit dtypes (float32 for large posterior arrays), chunked or streaming computation over posterior draws, scan-based likelihoods to bound device memory, parquet for processed data, categoricals for low-cardinality features. Do not materialise large broadcast arrays.
- **Style and quality:** NumPy-style docstrings, type hints (PEP 604 unions), ruff for linting and formatting, pre-commit hooks, pytest, GitHub Actions CI. MIT licence for the code.
- **Notebooks are thin:** orchestration, narrative and visualisation only, importing all real logic from the package. Pair notebooks with jupytext (.py) for clean diffs and commit them executed so results are visible on GitHub.
- **Commit history** should be incremental and legible.

## 5. Data

Region: NSW1 only (Sydney load centre). Demand resolution half-hourly. Weather hourly, interpolated onto the half-hourly grid in preprocessing.

### Demand

AEMO operational demand actuals for NSW1, from NEMWeb. Prefer a maintained Python accessor (for example NEMOSIS) for reproducibility, with a direct NEMWeb fetch as fallback. The data module must use operational demand specifically, which differs from total demand, and must document the exact series, report and resolution it uses.

### Weather

A single provider, Open-Meteo, supplies both ground truth and genuine forecasts through one clean JSON API, free for non-commercial use (CC BY 4.0 data, attribution required, which must appear in `data/README.md` and the project README).

- **Actuals (ground truth):** Historical Weather API (`/v1/archive`), ERA5 reanalysis, hourly, spatially complete. Used to train the demand-weather relationship and as the truth against which forecasts are scored.
- **Forecast as issued:** the archived operational forecasts, with the Bureau's ACCESS-G global model among the available models. For strict no-look-ahead backtesting use the Previous Runs API (fixed lead-time offsets of 1 to 7 days) or the Single Runs API (individual runs by initialisation time). Confirm per-model archive start dates: most from January 2024, GFS 2 m temperature from March 2021, and the historical forecast archive from roughly 2021 to 2022.

### Window

Governed by forecast-archive depth. Use a contiguous NSW1 window inside roughly 2022 to present, or 2024 to present if relying on the cleaner no-leakage APIs. Two to three years of half-hourly data is sufficient. ERA5 actuals extend back to 1940 and place no constraint on the actuals side.

### Known train/serve consideration

Weather coefficients are trained on ERA5 actuals, while operational forecasts come from ACCESS-G. The two carry different biases, so there is a mild train/serve mismatch. This is acceptable for the demonstration and should be acknowledged in the relevant notebook. Bias-correcting the forecast onto ERA5, or training directly on the historical-forecast archive, is noted as possible future work.

### Reproducibility

Download scripts fetch raw data into `data/raw` (gitignored). `data/README.md` documents provenance, the exact series, resolutions and licences. Processed splits are small and committed so reviewers see results without credentials.

## 6. Modelling

### 6.1 Classical baseline

Dynamic harmonic regression with ARIMA errors, via statsmodels SARIMAX. Exogenous regressors: Fourier harmonics for the daily cycle (period 48) and weekly cycle (period 336), optionally annual, plus weather terms (temperature and cooling and heating degree days). ARIMA(p, d, q) on the residual structure. Order selection on the validation set. The Gaussian predictive density yields an analytic CRPS, used for a fair probabilistic comparison and as a correctness check for the sample-based estimator. A bare ARIMA on raw half-hourly demand is deliberately avoided, as it would be a strawman given the multiple seasonalities and weather dependence.

### 6.2 Shared BSTS (defined once, consumed by both inference paths)

Defined in `models/bsts.py` as a single NumPyro model, written as an explicit state-space model using `scan`.

- **Trend:** stochastic local linear trend (level and slope), with an optional damped AR(1) slope for stability.
- **Seasonality:** default to static Fourier harmonic regression for the daily and weekly cycles, which keeps the latent state low-dimensional (only level and slope), RAM-friendly and well-identified. A time-varying (stochastic) trigonometric seasonal block is an enabled option if EDA shows seasonal-shape drift, implemented with non-centred innovations. Avoid dummy-variable seasonality, whose weekly block would be a 335-dimensional state.
- **Weather:** static regression coefficients with Gaussian priors (temperature, degree days, optional interactions).
- **Observation noise:** Gaussian by default, Student-t available as a robustness option for demand spikes.
- **Parameterisation:** non-centred throughout for all stochastic innovations and hierarchical scales, to defuse the funnel and give NUTS a tractable geometry.

Priors should be weakly informative and documented with their rationale.

### 6.3 Inference

**ADVI** (`inference_vi.py`): mean-field surrogate (`AutoNormal`) with full-rank (`AutoMultivariateNormal`) as a contrast. The training loop logs, per interval, the ELBO together with its energy and entropy components, where the ELBO equals the expected log joint (energy) plus the surrogate entropy. Prefer the closed-form Gaussian entropy where the guide provides it, and Monte-Carlo estimate the energy from surrogate draws. The mean-field-versus-full-rank contrast shows where the mean-field assumption under-represents posterior correlation and variance.

**NUTS** (`inference_mcmc.py`): multiple chains with full diagnostics, namely R-hat, bulk and tail ESS, divergences, E-BFMI and tree-depth saturation. Treated as the reference posterior.

### 6.4 ADVI warm start for NUTS

An optional `vi_init` argument to the NUTS fitter seeds two things from the fitted surrogate: the initial position of each chain (drawn from q), and the inverse mass matrix (from the surrogate covariance, diagonal from mean-field, dense from full-rank). Adaptation may then refine or be frozen.

Accounting must be honest:

- Cold total is warmup plus sampling, default initialisation, mass matrix learned from scratch.
- Warm total is ADVI fit plus reduced warmup plus sampling.
- Compare at matched quality, not matched iteration count: total wall-clock to reach a target bulk-ESS with R-hat below 1.01 and no divergences. A shorter warmup that mixes worse must not be reported as faster.
- Report the variants (diagonal metric from mean-field, dense metric from full-rank) and be prepared to show a null or negative result. If ADVI under-estimates variance, the derived metric can be miscalibrated and may not help, or may raise divergences. That finding, if it occurs, is reported plainly.

Pathfinder is noted as a principled alternative warm-start source (BlackJAX) that may be added later.

## 7. Forecast task, splits and weather-input variants

**Task:** day-ahead, 48 half-hourly steps.

**Splits:** chronological train, validation and test (roughly 70/15/15), no shuffling, no leakage. Rolling-origin evaluation across the test set for horizon-resolved scoring.

**Weather input.** Weather coefficients are trained on ERA5 actuals. At forecast time the covariate is supplied under three variants:

- **Real archived forecast (headline):** the honest operational setup, with no look-ahead bias.
- **ERA5 perfect foresight:** an explicit upper bound, disclosed as such.
- **Calibrated perturbation sweep (robustness):** the perturbation generator adds a lead-time-dependent, temporally correlated error (AR(1) or a GP over the horizon) to the actuals, with magnitude and correlation fitted to the measured forecast-minus-ERA5 residuals from the real archive. Sweeping input quality from perfect foresight to degraded shows how each model's accuracy and calibration hold up, and whether the Bayesian model is more robust to covariate error.

## 8. Evaluation

**Timing:** a `timed()` context manager records fit time and forecast time per model into the results. Under JAX, compile time and run time are recorded separately so timings are not inflated by XLA compilation. For NUTS, ESS/sec and time-to-target-ESS are reported in addition to wall-clock, since raw wall-clock is not quality-adjusted across stochastic samplers.

**Point metrics:** MAE, RMSE and MASE.

**Probabilistic metrics:** CRPS (primary), log predictive density, pinball loss across quantiles, central-interval coverage and PIT calibration. CRPS for the Bayesian models uses the sample-based energy-form estimator, computed streaming over draws. CRPS for the classical model uses the analytic Gaussian form. The sample estimator is unit-tested against the analytic Gaussian form in the Gaussian limit.

**Cross-method posterior comparison:** ADVI (mean-field and full-rank) against NUTS, quantifying mean-field variance under-estimation on marginals and key pairwise correlations.

**Computational comparison:** the master table in notebook 05 carries fit time, forecast time and, for NUTS, ESS/sec, alongside the scores.
